QUESTION 1
Describe a time you needed to collaborate and develop trust from a stakeholder
==========================================================================================

Situation
The Chat Analysis Tool (CAT) is used by internal business areas, including the Digital
Assistant team, to analyse customer interaction trends and inform evidence based
improvements to chatbot responses. By the time I picked up the work, confidence in the
tool had visibly eroded. The application was unstable, required frequent manual restarts,
and fell short of business expectations around user access and reliability. Users could
not depend on the tool when they actually needed it, and that frustration had settled into
a noticeable trust gap between the business and the delivery team.

Task
I took ownership of improving CAT and decided early that the technical fixes alone would
not be enough. To deliver something the business would genuinely rely on, I had to bring
the dissatisfied stakeholders into the work, understand the experience from their side,
and shape the solution alongside them rather than for them.

Action
I started by setting up a series of meetings with the affected business users and
listening carefully to what was actually getting in their way, rather than relying on
tickets or second hand summaries. Together we shaped a clear picture of what good looked
like from their side: stable access, role appropriate permissions, fewer disruptions, and
confidence that issues raised would be followed through.

I then worked collaboratively to refine the requirements for authentication and role based
access. As the build progressed I organised follow up sessions to walk the business
through the changes, invited their feedback openly, and adjusted the work in response so
the final version reflected what we had agreed together. That iterative loop changed the
tone of the work; it became a shared effort rather than a delivery being pushed at them.

To address the reliability concerns I introduced an automated monitoring job that improved
day to day availability and substantially reduced the manual restarts that had been
frustrating users. Throughout, I kept communication consistent and honest about progress,
risk and timeframes, so the business stayed close to the work rather than waiting on a
finished product.

Result
Stakeholder sentiment moved from open frustration to active confidence in the tool. CAT
became materially more stable, access reflected how the business actually operated, and
disruptions to daily work dropped away. The collaborative way the work was done also left
the relationship in a stronger place than before the issues started. The Digital Assistant
team now engages with CAT as a constructive partner, which has made subsequent
improvements noticeably easier to land.

QUESTION 2
Describe a time you helped someone understand the strategic direction of your area
or the business context of a decision
==========================================================================================

Situation
The Data Engineering Operations team supports data processes that must remain reliable,
traceable and maintainable for ongoing business use. The team was moving toward more
consistent delivery practices, including clearer documentation, shared development
standards and disciplined use of version control. When a senior technical team member left
the agency, the transition created a natural moment to strengthen the team's shared
understanding of why these practices mattered for safe and sustainable delivery.

Task
After joining the team and building my own understanding through handover discussions and
existing materials, I took on the task of helping colleagues see the business reasoning
behind the delivery practices being adopted, particularly around version control and the
GitLab workflow. Lifting the team's shared understanding of why the process existed would
directly support more consistent delivery, stronger operational reliability and a more
confident way of working across the group.

Action
I organised several team sessions where we worked through the GitLab workflow
collaboratively rather than as a one way explanation. We walked through how changes moved
from a working branch through review and approval before reaching the main branch, and I
supported colleagues where they had less exposure. Treating the sessions as a shared
discussion meant people brought real examples from their own work, which kept the
conversation practical and grounded.

I connected each practice back to a clear business outcome: version control protects
traceability of changes to production data processes, reviews on merge requests reduce the
risk of avoidable errors reaching downstream consumers, and disciplined branching makes
releases predictable and recoverable. Framed this way, the workflow became a delivery
safeguard in the team's eyes rather than overhead, and gave them a sounder basis for
judgement when situations arose that the documentation did not directly cover.

The sessions were recorded by the team for later reference, and I continued to support
colleagues informally as questions came up in day to day work.

Result
The team carried the work forward confidently through the staff transition, with knowledge
retained across the group. Delivery practice became more consistent, and colleagues were
better placed to apply judgement on new situations because they understood what the
workflow was protecting. The recorded sessions continued to be a useful reference for the
team. For me, the work strengthened my own grasp of how disciplined delivery supports
operational reliability and how to translate technical practice into business value.

QUESTION 3
Describe a time you led by demonstrating continuous growth, emotional intelligence
or respect
==========================================================================================

Situation
Soon after joining the Data Engineering Operations team, I picked up work on a GitLab
CI/CD pipeline for one of the team's database repositories. The pipeline was still
maturing and carried real quirks, including merge request templates not appearing as
expected and workflow rules behaving inconsistently across branches. The territory was
unfamiliar in places, for me and for some colleagues, and the way issues were handled
would shape how confident the team felt working in the pipeline going forward.

Task
I set out to build my own capability at pace and to lead by how I engaged with the work.
As I worked through real issues in the pipeline, I wanted to bring colleagues alongside
respectfully, keep the tone constructive when things went wrong, and leave the pipeline
and the team in a stronger position than I found them.

Action
I built my understanding deliberately. When the pipeline behaved unexpectedly, I read the
configuration carefully, traced how variables were scoped, and tested how rules fired
across branches before asking targeted questions. The MR templates, for example, were not
surfacing because the template files sat on a non default branch; in another case a
project level variable was quietly overriding the dynamic workflow rules. Working these
out cleanly, rather than guessing, made the explanations I later shared with colleagues
grounded and reliable.

How I engaged with people was equally deliberate. When a colleague hit something I had
already worked through, I walked them through the reasoning rather than only the fix, so
the understanding carried forward. When I was the one stuck, I said so plainly. I treated
problems as shared learning rather than individual mistakes, which kept conversations open
and meant issues were raised early rather than after they had grown. Where something was
likely to trip the next person up, I surfaced it for the team to learn from once.

Result
The pipeline became more reliable and easier to work in, and the team grew noticeably more
confident operating within it. Questions came up earlier, issues were resolved with less
friction, and the tone stayed constructive even under pressure. My own capability in
DevOps and CI/CD strengthened meaningfully alongside the team's, and the experience
reinforced for me that integrity and respect in how you show up shape the working
environment as much as what you deliver.

QUESTION 4
Describe a time you found a solution to a technical, ethical or complex problem
==========================================================================================

Situation
I worked on the Australian Government Disaster Recovery Payment project, where a machine
learning model had been introduced to support the existing fraud detection strategy. The
model was producing useful results, but its outputs were not being retained reliably once
the application moved beyond test conditions. The complexity sat in the constraints around
the fix. The output format and capture had to change without disturbing the surrounding
code structure or the model's existing behaviour, and the change had to hold up not only
in local testing but also in a production environment simulation under the volume of real
payment claim activity, where edge cases that never appear in isolated tests can surface
quickly.

Task
After an initial clarification with my manager to confirm the intent and boundaries of the
work, I took on the task of making the model's outputs reliably traceable in production,
deploying and validating the change in OpenShift, and reviewing package vulnerabilities to
support cyber security. The goal was to improve traceability and reliability without
introducing avoidable change or release risk into a model the business already relied on.

Action
I started by reframing the problem. The real requirement was not simply to produce model
results, but to ensure those results were retained in a form the business could trust and
trace later, while leaving the surrounding application and model behaviour untouched. That
framing shaped each decision that followed.

I reviewed the application flow to identify the right points to capture outputs, then
adjusted the output format and connected it to the appropriate storage, working within the
existing code structure rather than reshaping it. I deployed the change in OpenShift and
validated it end to end under a production environment simulation with realistic payment
claim volume, which surfaced behaviours that local testing alone would not have revealed.

Across the work I stayed close to the teams involved. I met with relevant teams for
clarification on operational expectations and to invite feedback on the captured results,
so the format and content of the logs reflected what would actually be useful downstream
rather than only what was technically convenient. In parallel I worked through the package
vulnerability review, weighing each required update against the risk of altering model
behaviour, and raised any ambiguity early rather than absorbing the decision alone.

Result
Model outputs are now reliably captured and traceable in production, closing a real gap in
the fraud detection pipeline. The change met cyber security expectations and was handed
over to the Applied Intelligence team in a state they could release with confidence. I
have continued supporting the work as further requirements have surfaced, which has helped
keep the solution maintainable rather than becoming fragile over time. The experience
reinforced for me the value of framing a problem precisely, validating under realistic
conditions, and staying close to the teams who rely on the output, particularly in
production settings where small misjudgements carry disproportionate cost.
